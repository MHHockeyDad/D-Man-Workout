⚠ Youth Athlete Safety Guidelines
  • All strength work uses light loads — technique and range of motion always come first. Form over weight, every time.
  • Never skip warm-up or cool-down. Growing bodies need proper prep and recovery.
  • Rest days are training days. Sleep 9–10 hours. Hydrate continuously.
  • If anything hurts (not just "burns") — STOP. Pain is a signal, not a challenge.
  • Adjust weights based on feel: if form breaks down, drop the weight immediately.
  • Always have an adult present for free weight exercises.
01
Program StructureTHREE-PHASE PROGRESSION
Phase I — Weeks 1–4
Foundation & Motor Patterns
WEEKS 1 – 4
Build movement vocabulary. A-skips, B-skips, carioca, ladder fundamentals, hip flexor activation, body weight strength. Learn how to accelerate and decelerate correctly on blades.
Phase II — Weeks 5–8
Speed & Power Development
WEEKS 5 – 8
Increase intensity and complexity. Introduce light dumbbell work, explosive starts, lateral quickness, extended roller blade sessions, and first-step acceleration patterns.
Phase III — Weeks 9–12
Competition Prep & Peak
WEEKS 9 – 12
Peak speed, max agility complexity, progressive strength loads, longer endurance intervals, and game-speed pattern work. Sharpen everything built in Phases I and II.
Equipment Used in This Program

Cones · Agility Ladder · Dumbbells (light — 5 lb, 8 lb, 10 lb max) · Free Weights / Barbell (bodyweight to 15 lb bar only) · Roller Blades · Bike

  • Stay hydrated — 60+ oz water/day

Reference GuideDRILL LIBRARY
Skip Mechanics
A-Skip
March with exaggerated knee drive. Swing opposite arm forward. Drive the ball of foot straight down. This builds sprint mechanics and hip flexor activation.
Skip Mechanics
B-Skip
Start with an A-skip, then extend the raised leg fully forward before driving the foot down and back. Trains the full sprint leg cycle — hamstring loading and pawing action.
Skip Mechanics
C-Skip (Lateral)
A lateral version of the A-skip. Drive the inside knee up and across while moving sideways. Builds hip abductor and adductor strength critical for lateral hockey movement.
Skip Mechanics
Power Skip
Maximum height single-leg bound. Drive the knee up, push off the ground explosively, get maximum air time. Builds single-leg power and coordination.
Agility
T-Drill
Sprint 10 yards, shuffle left 5 yards, shuffle right 10 yards, shuffle back left 5 yards, backpedal to start. One of the best measures of multi-directional speed.
Agility
5-10-5 (Pro Agility)
From center, sprint 5 yards right, touch line, sprint 10 yards left, touch line, sprint 5 yards back to center. Tests change of direction and deceleration.
Agility
Carioca
Moving laterally with alternating crossover steps — right foot crosses in front, then behind, as you move sideways. Builds hip mobility, coordination, and lateral foot speed.
Hip Flexor
Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch
Drop to one knee, drive hips forward while keeping core braced. Add an overhead reach on the kneeling side for a full psoas stretch. Hold 60–90 seconds each side.
Hip Flexor
90/90 Hip Stretch
Sit on floor with both knees at 90° — one in front, one to the side. Lean gently over the front leg. Works deep hip rotators and hip flexors simultaneously.
Hip Flexor
Supine Knee Drive
Lying on back, alternate driving one knee toward chest while the other leg stays extended just above the floor. Activates hip flexors without lower back strain.
Strength
Goblet Squat
Hold one dumbbell at chest height, feet shoulder-width. Sit into a full squat keeping elbows inside knees. Perfect introductory squat for young athletes — builds legs and core together.
Strength
Romanian Deadlift (RDL)
Hold dumbbells in front of thighs, hinge at hips with soft knees until you feel a hamstring stretch, then drive hips forward to stand. Builds posterior chain — essential for skating power.
Strength
Nordic Hamstring Curl
Kneel with feet anchored. Slowly lower your body toward the floor using only hamstring tension — use hands to catch yourself at the bottom. Exceptional injury prevention for skaters.
Ladder
Icky Shuffle
Step into the ladder with left foot, then right foot, then step left foot outside to the left, move to next rung and repeat. One of the highest-coordination ladder patterns — crucial for hockey foot speed.
Single-Leg Jumps
Single-Leg Hop & Stick
Jump off one foot and land on the same foot. Freeze for 1–2 seconds before the next rep — don't rush. The "stick" (balance hold) is the most important part. This is the foundation for all single-leg jump work.
Single-Leg Jumps
Single-Leg Broad Jump
Take off from one foot, drive the knee up and reach forward, land on the same foot with a soft bent knee. Hold the landing 1–2 seconds. Measures single-leg horizontal power — tracked at Weeks 4, 8, and 12 to show progress.
Single-Leg Jumps
Single-Leg Vertical Jump
Drive off one foot straight up, swing the free knee upward for height, land back on the same foot with control. Touch a wall target to track height. Builds the same single-leg push-off used in a skating stride.
Single-Leg Jumps
Single-Leg Lateral Bound
Push off one foot sideways, land on the opposite foot and hold. For consecutive reps, immediately bound back. Mimics the lateral push-off in a skating crossover — one of the most hockey-specific power movements in the program.
Single-Leg Jumps
Single-Leg Depth Landing
Step off a low platform (6–8") and land on ONE foot — soft knee, quiet landing, hold for 2 seconds. This is pure force absorption training. It teaches the ankle, knee, and hip to work together under impact — critical for injury prevention in skaters.

Fueling PerformanceNUTRITION BASICS
🍽 Fueling a Growing Hockey Player
  • Pre-training (60–90 min before): Carbs + small protein — oatmeal, toast with peanut butter, banana + yogurt
  • Post-training (within 30 min): Protein + carbs — chocolate milk, turkey sandwich, eggs + toast, Greek yogurt + fruit
  • Daily protein target: ~0.7g per pound of bodyweight (~52g/day). Chicken, eggs, dairy, beans, fish
  • Hydration: 60–80 oz water daily minimum. Add more on training days. No energy drinks.
  • Sleep: 9–10 hours every night. This is where growth hormone releases and muscles rebuild.
